Retention Analysis Dashboard — the reconciliation

The shadow-calculation trap made visible: department self-reports vs. the official IPEDS-aligned metric, the at-risk students with no intervention on file, and the equity trend. Built on an embedded snapshot so it opens instantly.

Why the two numbers differ: the department averages each cohort's rate, giving every cohort equal weight regardless of size, so a tiny 90%-retained group counts the same as a large one in the 50s. The governed metric divides total retained students by the total eligible base — one enrollment-weighted definition. The gap isn't a data error; it's the aggregation being defined in the wrong place. A precomputed rate is locked to one grain, so every tool re-derives its own answer. Mosaic owns the rule once and recomputes it correctly at any grain, so the number can't drift between the department, the dashboard and the board.

The retention-risk model

0.695ROC-AUC

ROC-AUC measures how well the model ranks students: pick one who dropped and one who didn't at random, and 0.695 is the probability the model scores the dropout higher. 0.50 is a coin flip, 1.00 is perfect separation — 0.695 is a real, honest signal for a synthetic cohort, good enough to rank risk, not to pretend precision.

Wrap-up

The verdict.

Overall assessment across all six building blocks: does a governed semantic layer actually fix the higher-ed trust deficit?

Trust starts with a single definition.

The most important takeaway from the demo isn't the retention rate itself. It's the difference between what departments report and what the governed metric shows. In the demo, departments estimate retention at nearly 90%, while the IPEDS-aligned metric is in the mid-50s. That difference illustrates the trust problem many institutions face when every department calculates the same metric differently.

  • ✓One definition across every experience. The presentation, dashboard, advisor workspace, and AI applications all use the same governed metric. Changing the interface doesn't change the number.
  • ✓The equity trend is consistent. The achievement gap declines from 18.0 to 16.8 to 15.4 percentage points across three cohorts because every analysis is based on the same underlying definition.
  • ✓AI uses governed metrics, not its own calculations. Mosaic provides the deterministic definitions for metrics and business logic, while AI provides probabilistic predictions and recommendations. An AI assistant can explain why a student is considered high risk, but it references the governed retention metric and the model's output instead of generating its own numbers.
  • ✓The results are actionable. The dashboard identifies 97 high-risk students without a recorded intervention, giving advisors a concrete list to prioritize.

The larger point is that AI is inherently probabilistic. It estimates outcomes based on patterns and confidence levels. Institutional metrics, however, need to be deterministic so every report, dashboard, and application returns the same answer to the same question. Mosaic provides that deterministic foundation, allowing AI to generate predictions and recommendations without changing the underlying business definitions.
